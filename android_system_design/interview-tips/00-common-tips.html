Common Tips — All Interview Rounds

These apply regardless of round type. Internalize them so they're automatic — don't spend mental bandwidth on these during the actual interview.

Before You Answer Anything

Tip 01 · Clarify before solving

Spend 60–90 seconds asking clarifying questions before drawing anything or writing any code. Interviewers score this positively — it shows you don't jump to solutions before understanding the problem. At L6, unclear requirements are a trap you should recognize.

"Before I start, let me make sure I understand the constraints. [Ask 2–3 questions.] And just to confirm — is [X] in scope, or should I treat that as a future extension?"
Tip 02 · State your assumptions out loud

If you assume something (e.g., 10M DAU, mobile-only, English-only), say it explicitly before building on it. If your assumption is wrong, the interviewer will correct you. If it's right, you've demonstrated structured thinking.

"I'm going to assume we're targeting ~10M DAU to start, and the primary client is Android. Let me know if those don't match what you have in mind."
Tip 03 · Take thinking time — out loud

Silence feels longer to you than to the interviewer. But rather than silent pauses, think out loud: narrate your reasoning even when you're uncertain. "Let me think about this for a second — the key constraint here is..." keeps the interviewer engaged and shows your thought process.

During the Answer

Tip 04 · Name trade-offs before being asked

Every significant decision has an alternative. Name both options and explain why you chose one given this specific context. This is the single most consistent L6 differentiator across all round types.

"I could go with [A] or [B] here. [A] gives me [benefit] but costs [downside]. Given that [specific constraint from this problem], I'll go with [A]. If [constraint] changed, I'd revisit."
Tip 05 · Flag failure modes proactively

Before describing the happy path, spend 30 seconds listing what can go wrong. Interviewers are specifically listening for this. "What happens when this fails?" is the question they're waiting to ask — answer it before they do.

"Before I describe the success path, let me think about failure modes. The main things that can break here are [X], [Y], and [Z]. Here's how I'd handle each..."
Tip 06 · Engage with pushback — don't fold or fight

When the interviewer challenges your design, they're probing your reasoning, not trying to trick you. Don't cave immediately ("you're right, I'll change it") and don't dig in defensively. Engage: acknowledge what's valid in their concern, update if warranted, and explain your reasoning.

"That's a good point. You're right that [concern] is real. I was optimizing for [X] — if [Y] matters more in this context, then [specific change]. Does that address it, or is there something else I'm missing?"
Tip 07 · Be honest about what you don't know

Faking confidence on gaps is worse than admitting them. L6 engineers know the boundaries of their knowledge. Acknowledge the gap, reason from first principles, and say what you'd do to validate.

"I haven't used [X] in production, but reasoning from what I know about [related concept] — the constraint here is [Y], so the solution needs to satisfy [Z]. My best guess is [answer]. I'd prototype and validate before committing."
Tip 08 · Give concrete numbers

Generic answers ("it's fast", "it's scalable") signal L4. Specific numbers signal L6: "P95 under 200ms", "cache hit rate target 80%", "retry with 500ms base delay and 50ms jitter". Even if your numbers are approximate, being concrete shows production experience.

Tip 09 · Bring the interviewer with you

Check in every 5–7 minutes: "Does this make sense so far?" or "Should I go deeper here or move on?" This catches misunderstandings early, prevents you from over-investing in the wrong direction, and demonstrates collaborative communication.

"That covers the data layer — does this make sense before I move to the interface design, or would you like me to go deeper on any part of it?"

What to Say When Things Go Wrong

Tip 10 · Catching yourself mid-answer

If you realize you've been answering at the wrong level (too detailed, too vague, wrong direction), self-correct out loud. This is a positive signal, not a failure.

"Actually, let me step back — I've been going into implementation details before establishing the system boundaries. Let me draw the component structure first, then we can go into the detail."
Tip 11 · When you make a mistake

Acknowledge it and move forward. Don't dwell on it, don't over-apologize. Interviewers are not scoring perfection — they're scoring how you think and recover.

"Actually, that's wrong — I said [X] but the problem with that is [Y]. The better approach is [Z] because..."
Tip 12 · When you're stuck

Narrate your thinking process. Interviewers can give hints to candidates who show they're thinking — they can't help candidates who go quiet. Show your reasoning even when you don't have the answer yet.

"I'm not immediately sure of the answer here. Let me reason through it: the constraint is [X], which means [Y]. If I approach it from [angle], I get [result]. That suggests [direction] — does that seem right to you?"

Timing and Pacing

Tip 13 · Watch the clock, not the topic

Most candidates run out of time before finishing. Timebox each phase and move on even if you'd like to go deeper. A complete 45-minute answer at a shallower depth scores higher than a deep 30-minute answer that leaves 3 sections untouched.

Tip 14 · Compress early sections to protect the Staff+ layer

Requirements + Architecture tend to expand to fill all available time. The observability, rollout, and security sections at the end are where L6 is differentiated. If you're running long, compress architecture — not the final sections.

Tip 15 · End with open questions, not a period

When you've covered the key material, flag what you'd explore next rather than just stopping. This shows engineering depth and that you can identify what's unresolved.

"I've covered the core design. The areas I'd want to go deeper on if we had more time are: [1], [2], and [3]. Which would you like to explore?"

Things Never to Say

Never say these
  • "It depends." — Say what it depends on, immediately.
  • "That's a good question." — Filler. Just answer.
  • "In my current company..." — Fine as an example; not fine as a reason. Say why it's right for this problem.
  • "I think I'd..." — Use "I would" — hedging language signals uncertainty about things you should be certain about.
  • "I'm not sure if this is right but..." — Frame uncertainty with a plan: "I'd validate this by..."
  • "Is that what you're looking for?" — You're not guessing. Ask a specific clarifying question instead.
